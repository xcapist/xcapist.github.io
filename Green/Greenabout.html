About The "Pepperhead"

Freddie Green was Born in Charleston, South Carolina on March 31, 1911. He was influenced by music at a very young age, leaning the banjo in his early years before taking up the guitar. Sam Walker, a family friend to his father, taught Green to read music and would encourage him to continue playing, even getting Green his first gig with the local community group he organized. After losing his parents, he began to play around in city clubs to make money and would be introduced to Count Basie, whom he would play with for the next 50 years. Green prided himself on his ability to play rhythm guitar, and rarely performed a solo. He had a unique style of play, comping, that helped emphasize the drums and was adamant that the guitar should be accompanied, not standing out. "You should never hear the guitar by itself. It should be part of the drums so it sounds like the drummer is playing chords - like the snare is in A or the hi-hat in D minor."

    • One of few sucessful Solo Guitarist and Male Vocalist in the early Commercial Recording Industry

  • Awards

    • His 1927 recording of "Matchbox Blues" is a part of the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame's 500 Songs that Shaped Rock and Roll
    • Inducted into the Blues Hall of Fame in 1980

  • Interesting Facts

    • Played with the Count Basie Orchestra for 50 years
    • Member to one of the most respected rhythm sections in big band history (Basie band 1937)
    • Created two head arrangements: Corner Pocket and Down for Double
    • Published under the pseudonym Deacon L. J. Bates early in his career before using his real name
